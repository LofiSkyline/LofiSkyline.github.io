Core Foundation of CIA Data Protection and Risk Assessment

Core Foundation of CIA Data Protection and Risk Assessment

(CIA 数据保护与风险评估的核心基础)

When designing secure systems, the first step is to clarify the protection goals. The traditional CIA Triad is the foundation for measuring security properties.

1. The CIA Triad and Case Studies

  • Confidentiality (机密性): Can this data only be read by authorized actors?
    • Case Study: Market-affecting information (可能影响市场的机密信息). If leaked, it could lead to illegal insider dealing (内幕交易).
  • Integrity (完整性): Can this data only be changed by authorized actors?
    • Case Study: Bonus payments spreadsheet (奖金计算表). If integrity is compromised, employees could illegally alter amounts for personal gain.
  • Availability (可用性): Is this data always available to authorized actors?
    • Case Study: Whistle-blower data (举报人提供的证据). If data is deleted or temporarily unavailable, involved parties might escape accountability.

2. Impact Levels (影响等级)

The consequences of compromised security properties are typically quantified through Impact Levels (usually on a scale of 1-5 or 1-6).

  • Level 1: No or trivial impact (无影响或轻微影响).
  • Level 5/6: Major loss of life or vast economic harm (重大损失、生命危险或巨大的经济破坏).

UK Official Classification Mapping (CIA Triples)

Although these specific classifications are now partially deprecated in the UK, the numerical ratings system remains widely used:

  • 334: RESTRICTED (受限级)
  • 444: CONFIDENTIAL (秘密级)
  • 554: SECRET (机密级)
  • 664: TOP SECRET (绝密级)

Specific Impact Cases (IL 3 vs IL 6)

  • IL 3: Risk to an individual’s personal safety or liberty; Loss to HMG/Public Sector of £millions; Undermine the financial viability of a minor UK-based organisation.
  • IL 6: Lead directly to widespread loss of life; Major, long-term damage to the UK economy (in excess of £10 billion); Prolonged recession or hyperinflation.

Risk Analysis and Control Framework

(风险分析与控制框架)

The core of security management lies in thinking adversarially (对抗性思维)—putting yourself in the shoes of both the attacker and the defender.

1. Analyzing Adversaries (分析攻击者)

When evaluating attackers, consider three dimensions:

  • Skilled (技能): What is their level of technical expertise?
  • Motivated (动机): What is their willingness to break the law, take risks, and what is the size of the possible pay-off?
  • Resourced (资源): How much funding, computing power, or manpower do they have?
  • Common types of attackers: Fraudsters (诈骗者), business competitors, disgruntled employees, foreign governments.

2. Designing Controls (制定控制措施)

When designing countermeasures (Controls), you must balance:

  • Cost (成本): Expenses for implementation and maintenance.
  • Intrusive (侵入性): Does it disrupt normal business operations?
  • Acceptance (用户接受度): Will users accept them, or will they bypass security because it’s too inconvenient?
  • Unintended consequences (意外后果): Will the countermeasures introduce new problems?

Evolution from “Craft” to “Repeatable Processes”

(从“手工艺”到“可重复流程”的演变)

The professor uses WWII manufacturing to illustrate the origins and core logic of modern quality management systems like ISO 27001.

1. WWII Case Study: Munitions Manufacturing (军火制造)

  • Background: Wartime required producing dangerous munitions in high volume by untrained staff under high-pressure, less-than-ideal conditions.
  • Transition: Security and quality could no longer rely on individual craft (手艺); they had to rely on repeatable processes (可重复的流程).
  • Logic: If the process works, testing one item implies a reasonable chance the others will work too. Testing becomes process control, not just quality control. Consistency is achieved through the process.

2. Prescriptive Standards vs. Quality Standards

  • Prescriptive Standards (规定性标准): e.g., BS57 bolt standard. Precisely defines every detail (sizes, tolerances) to ensure interchangeability, but gives little flexibility. Can be objectively enforced by QA processes and measurement.
  • Quality Standards (质量标准): e.g., ISO 27001. Describes how to do things (the approach/meta-process) rather than what exactly to do. It is enforced by auditing documentation and records, making it much more flexible.

ISMS Management Model: From Ad hoc to PDCA

(ISMS 管理模式)

1. Ad hoc Security (即兴安全)

An unsystematic approach where management fixes things as they think of them. They often chase the exciting, interesting, high-profile stuff (like advanced cryptography) and ignore the boring, mundane, quotidian (日常琐碎的) tasks.

2. The Cycle of Quality Systems / Deming Cycle (PDCA 戴明环)

This is the core of establishing Managed Security:

  • Plan (计划): Identify assets (Asset Register), risks, threats, and associate controls.
  • Do (实施): Run the system with the new controls applied and generate Quality Records.
  • Check (检查): Design and collect metrics and audit information; assess the success of the system.
  • Act (改进): Improve the system and return to “Do” or “Plan”.

Document Governance Framework

(文档治理框架)

A complete ISMS relies on a hierarchical document system:

Level Definition Written By Approved By
Policies (策略) State the objectives and criteria of the system (“how things should be”). Clear, short in content, long in duration. Senior Managers Board-level Directors
Procedures (流程) Step-by-step descriptions of how to do something correctly. Checked against policy. Operational Managers Line Managers / Policy Owners
Work Instructions (工作指令) More detailed method statements for specific tasks. Technical Experts Operational Managers
Quality Records (质量记录) Evidence that procedures are followed (e.g., signed checklists), which can be audited. Staff / System System / Managers

Case Study: Warning on Language in University of Birmingham Policy

  • The Issue: An older IT Security policy defined its scope as “Members of the University” (大学成员).
  • The Risk: “Members” is legally defined in Statutes to include the Council, all students, and even honorary graduates (like comedian Lenny Henry). Enforcing compulsory security training on global alumni is impossible, leading to a massive audit failure.
  • The Fix: The policy was updated to apply to “all persons and corporate entities who access the University’s data or computing and network facilities”. This definition based on access behavior rather than identity tags is much better and auditable.

Asset Register: The Core of Management

(资产登记表)

The Asset Register is not just a hardware inventory; it is the cornerstone of defining the Trusted Computing Base and overall security planning.

1. Diversity of Assets (资产的多样性)

Assets include:

  • Physical Assets: Laptops, servers, data centers.
  • Intangible Assets: Datasets (HR, financial), applications.
  • Infrastructure: UPS (Uninterruptible Power Supply), diesel generators, air conditioning, Active Directory.
  • People: Key personnel are also vital system assets.

2. Dependency Hierarchy (依赖层级)

Assets have complex dependencies:
Datasets -> Databases -> Web Servers -> Virtualization Platform -> Hardware -> Power/HVAC -> Building.
The Asset Register must map these dependencies. Otherwise, if an underlying facility (like HVAC) fails, you may not foresee the crash of a top-level business process (like financial trading).

3. Key Benefit: Cleansing Activity (净化活动)

Building an asset register is an opportunity to clean up organizational entropy.

  • Case Study: The professor mentioned a Sun 10/514 server. Legacy systems often run in corners for years without anyone knowing their purpose. The register helps identify and decommission them, reducing the attack surface and costs.
  • Safe Harbor (避风港效应): In the event of a breach, demonstrating compliance with your own well-documented, asset-based processes is often enough to prove due diligence and avoid massive fines.

Metrics and Audit: Closing the Loop

(指标与审计：闭环验证)

The final step in the ISMS pipeline ensures that the system is not only implemented but is actually effective and sensible.

1. Metrics (指标收集与有效性)

The core purpose of metrics is to prove to management that the security investment is worthwhile and defending against real threats.

  • Vanity Metrics vs. Actionable Metrics: Avoid meaningless data like “firewall blocked 1 million automated scans”. Instead, focus on actionable trends, such as “phishing email click-rate dropped from 15% to 2% after training”.
  • The Problem of Non-Events: Security often deals with extreme, rare events (like a data center fire or a massive breach). Since these don’t happen often, you cannot rely on disaster statistics alone. Instead, metrics should track process performance (e.g., success rates of disaster recovery drills, time taken to patch critical vulnerabilities).

2. Internal vs. External Audit (内部审计与外部审计)

Auditing ensures that the rules are followed and that the rules make sense.

  • Internal Audit (内部审计): Conducted by internal staff. Its primary job is to check if employees are strictly adhering to the company’s own Procedures and generating the correct Quality Records (e.g., checking if onboarding checklists are signed).
    • Limitation: Internal audit only checks compliance with your own rules. If your policy wrongly states “we have no valuable data, so we need zero security”, internal audit will still pass you as long as you followed that rule perfectly.
  • External Audit (外部审计): Conducted by an independent third-party organization (e.g., BSI issuing an ISO 27001 certificate).
    • Purpose: It not only checks execution but verifies if your entire ISMS is sensible and adheres to industry norms. External auditors will immediately challenge a ridiculous policy.
    • Business Value: External certification is often the only way to prove security competence to clients and partners in B2B environments.

Key Terminology (关键术语)

  • ISMS: Information Security Management System (信息安全管理体系)
  • PDCA: Plan-Do-Check-Act (戴明环)
  • Asset Register: 资产登记表
  • Quality Records: 质量记录
  • Quotidian: Everyday/mundane (日常的/平凡的)
  • Safe Harbor: 避风港
